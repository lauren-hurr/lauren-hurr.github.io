Overview

Planit is an app that brings people together in-person or virtually by allowing users to create and invite their friends to events, ranging from casual dinners to formal parties. Invitees don’t need an account to RSVP to events, and the in-app reservation feature makes it easy to designate the meeting place.

Ever since I deleted most of my social media, I always felt out of the loop from my friends. I no longer received invitations to events or would have to be informally invited through text. In this project, I wanted to explore ways that I could stay connected without a social media account such as Facebook.

The Challenge

How might we shift user’s perception of coordinating events to be positive and convenient?

01.


Research

Empathizing with Users

User Interviews

After conducting 5 user interviews, I recognized there was no easy way to plan casual or formal events without a social media account or outdated online invitation platforms. Through user interviews, I wanted to identify pain points by analyzing users’ past experiences with event planning. I spent time learning what tools they had at their disposal and what specific struggles they encountered while organizing small to large events.

Are People Still Using Facebook to Create Events?

Consumer Research

I conducted consumer research to analyze how people communicate and stay social. Through this, I discovered that Facebook’s event planning feature is commonly used, but requires an account. Since data shows many people have deleted Facebook over the past years, it is no longer an effective way to plan and invite non-users.

I got mixed answers about Facebook usage, so I wanted validate these assumption by gathering data about social media usage. I found several interesting metrics:

  • 66% of users are using Facebook less often
  • Overall Facebook usage as a % of population in the U.S. is falling among younger people while increasing among older people.

02.


Synthesize

Extracting Actionable Insights from Data

Affinity Mapping, User Persona

I used an affinity map to organize the data from the user interviews so that I can uncover common user pain points, as well determine priorities moving forward. By breaking down the steps of event planning, I was able to uncover primary users' needs. The user persona was then crafted based on these goals and needs to represent who I will be designing for. This helped make sure that the decisions I made moving forward are user-centered. Creating a user persona also helped me make informed decisions when identifying features needed for the MVP.

03.


Ideate

Crafting the Solution

HMW/POV, Competitive analysis, Feature Roadmap

I used How Might We/Point of View (HMW/POV) questions to frame the problems found from research for ideation. This helped with the brainstorming process as restructuring user pain points into POV/HMW statements helped to clearly address the core problems found through research. During this process, I completed a competitive analysis on direct and indirect competitors to evaluate their strengths and weaknesses and to see how my new app would fill in any gaps in the market. After I brainstormed and selected the most practical solutions to solve the core user pain points, I created a feature roadmap for the MVP. My goal was to list out the minimum features that are necessary to test the viability of the app.

Main Features:

  • Booking a venue/restaurant
  • Messaging
  • Creating an event
  • RSVPs through text

Building Out a Strong Foundation

Site Map

To establish the blueprint of the app, I created a Site Map. I established this architecture by brainstorming how users would access each of the main features from the feature roadmap, and it helped me organize the screens in an intuitive fashion to make the event planning process logically sound and easy.

Establishing the Flow

Task Flow, User Flows, Sketches

The site map laid the foundation for the flow of the app, and I wanted to focus on having each essential feature seamlessly flow together. Based on the user persona's goals, I created a task flow to lay out the steps the user persona would take when planning an event. The user flow was then developed to organize the order of the tasks and decisions the user would take, and which respective screens the user would interact with. This also helped me break out different possible scenarios that the user might encounter. After I established the task flow and user flow, I began sketching out ideas of how the screens will look like. This helped me to rapidly examine my ideas before digitizing the designs.

04.


Prototype

Transforming Sketches into Mid-Fidelity Prototypes

Mid-Fi Prototype

After I finalized my sketches, I began focusing on visual consistency and hierarchy by turning them into mid-fidelity prototypes. The mid-fi prototypes were mainly created in order to test the viability of the app before establishing styles and visual design.

05.


Test

Catching Usability Bugs Early in the Design Process

Mid-Fi Prototype Usability Test

I wanted to discover usability issues early on before spending too much time on the visual details. I specifically looked for issues with feature placements, interruptions of the flow, and issues with navigation. During testing, I also evaluated the viability of the app by paying close attention to users' reactions and commentary. Overall, I found that users expressed positive sentiments about the app concept, but ran into a few usability issues in which I directly address in the redesigns.

Creating and Testing the Hi-Fi Prototype

Hi-Fidelity Prototyping, Usability Testing

I created the hi-fidelity prototype from the revised mid-fi prototype and used the style guide that I developed. I tested the hi-fi prototype with 7 participants to discover any other usability issues and whether or not my redesigns solved the issues discovered in the first test.

Validating the Redesigns

Hi-Fidelity Prototyping Results

I found that 100% of the usability issues found from the previous usability testing were resolved. However, I found a few other minor usability issues, which were quick fixes. Testing the hi-fidelity also allowed me to understand how users felt about the UI components and the overall impressions the app left.

Efficient Onboarding

Before making any commitment, the user sees the onboarding flow to understand what features the app provides and how it can benefit them. The user also has the option to skip the sign up/log in process. Since the app uses the user’s phone number for the account, no password creation is needed!

Quickly Create Events

It can take less than a minute to create an event, book a venue, and invite guests! Invites are sent through text, which yield faster responses than email. Also, the RSVP deadline option will encourage higher and faster response rates.

Explore and Reserve a Venue

The Planit app allows users to explore and reserve venues or restaurants that can accommodate their party without leaving the app. This eliminates the stress of doing outside research, contacting venues, and making reservations through third-party sites.

Manage RSVPs and Communicate With Guests

Notifications allow users to be updated on RSVPs as well as other activities that take place within their event page. The user can also receive direct messages from guests to minimize the need for communication outside of the app.

Reflection

This was the first time I created an end-to-end app, so it came with a unique set of challenges. One of the challenges was identifying what key features would be necessary for the MVP. It was difficult to only include the bare minimum as I wanted to include a variety of other features that I thought would be beneficial for the user. In the future, I would like to work in a lean UX process, since it focuses on creating an MVP as quickly as possible without focusing on deliverables. It would be great to see how different the product would look when taking a different approach.
